Under rule‑of‑capture, pumps race to the bottom — Can a cap‑and‑recharge compact fix it?

A (Problem): Texas Panhandle pumps under rule‑of‑capture shrink the Ogallala and dry domestic wells.
B (Objective): Lock in long‑term water security for farms, towns, and springs.
Link: A 3‑district cap‑and‑recharge compact: metered wells, tradable rights auctions, and playa‑basin recharge funded by satellite‑verified soil‑moisture credits.

Core intent: Will irrigators and GCD boards accept metering and a shared cap if rights are bankable/tradable and auction revenue visibly restores local recharge to stabilize wells and springs without collapsing farm income?

1) Opening problem map — Rule‑of‑capture reality

Three districts (High Plains UWCD, North Plains GCD, Panhandle GCD), major towns (Hereford, Dumas, Pampa), ring of domestic wells.

  • Avg Ogallala decline: 1.6 ft/yr
  • Domestic‑well outages (late summer): 20%
  • Pumped in study area: 150,000 AF/yr

2) Compact set‑up — Cap + rights + guardrails

  • Regional cap (year 1): 135,000 AF (−10% from baseline), glidepath −2%/yr for 5 yrs.
  • Allocations: 80% historic‑use baselines to existing meters; 20% auctioned annually.
  • Guardrails: 0.5‑mi domestic buffer; spring trigger if baseflow < 90% of 5‑yr median; max field drawdown 3 ft/yr.
Equity: 5% of auction volume reserved for new/young farmers.

5) Recharge marketplace — Turning revenue into water

  • Playas: 110 candidates; 60 restored this year @ $22k → $1.32M.
  • Satellite‑verified infiltration after two storms: +6" over 9,000 acres.
  • Credits minted: 9,000 × 0.5 AF/ac = 4,500 AF recharge credits.
  • Credit price: $150/AF paid to landowner co‑ops → $675k.
  • Verification: 12 basins spot‑checked; variance < 8% vs satellite.
